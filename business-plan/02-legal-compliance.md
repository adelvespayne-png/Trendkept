# 02 — Legal & Compliance (read this twice)

> This is the file that keeps you out of trouble. In supplements, the
> business-ending mistakes are almost always legal, not commercial. **None of
> this is legal advice** — you must retain a qualified attorney and (ideally) a
> regulatory consultant before selling. This file tells you what to ask them
> and what *not* to do in the meantime.

---

## 2.1 The regulatory landscape

### United States
- **Dietary supplements are regulated by the FDA** under **DSHEA** (Dietary
  Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994), as foods — **not** as drugs.
  They are **not** pre-approved by the FDA. The manufacturer/marketer is
  responsible for safety and truthful labeling.
- **cGMP (21 CFR Part 111):** manufacturers must follow current Good
  Manufacturing Practices. **Your supplier must be cGMP-compliant.** This is
  non-negotiable (see file 03).
- **FTC** polices advertising — all claims must be **truthful and substantiated**
  *before* you make them. The FTC is aggressive about supplement claims.
- **FDA facility registration:** the *manufacturer* registers their facility.
  Understand whether any registration obligations fall on you as the
  brand/distributor — ask your attorney.
- **New Dietary Ingredients (NDI):** stick to well-established, widely-sold
  ingredients. Novel ingredients can trigger NDI notification requirements —
  avoid them at launch.
- **Prop 65 (California):** certain warnings may be required. Your manufacturer
  and attorney advise here.

### UK / EU (if selling there)
- Supplements regulated as **food**; in the UK, **FSA**; advertising overseen by
  the **ASA/CAP code**; medicines line policed by the **MHRA**.
- **EU permitted health claims** are a *closed list* — you may only use claims
  on the authorised register, with exact approved wording. This is stricter
  than the US.
- VAT registration, and post-Brexit import/labeling rules, apply.

> **Decision:** Launch in **one** jurisdiction first (recommend your home
> market) to keep the compliance surface small. Expand only after the model
> works.

## 2.2 The claims rules — the single most common way to get fined

This deserves its own section because **illegal claims are the #1 enforcement
trigger** and the easiest mistake to make.

### NEVER say (these are illegal "disease claims" that make it an unapproved drug):
- "Cures / treats / prevents / diagnoses [any disease]."
- "Lowers blood pressure," "treats anxiety," "cures insomnia," "fights cancer,"
  "reverses diabetes," "kills the virus," etc.
- Anything implying it replaces medication or medical care.
- Before/after photos implying disease treatment; testimonials that make a
  disease claim you couldn't make yourself.

### Permitted (in the US): "structure/function" claims — but with conditions
- Allowed style: *"Supports a healthy sleep cycle,"* *"Helps maintain normal
  muscle function,"* *"Supports immune health."*
- Must be **truthful, not misleading, and substantiated** by competent evidence.
- US structure/function claims require the **FDA disclaimer**:
  > *"This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug
  > Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or
  > prevent any disease."*
- You must **notify the FDA** of structure/function claims (typically within 30
  days of first marketing) — confirm the current procedure with your attorney.

### Operating rule
> **Every piece of customer-facing copy** — ads, product pages, emails, social,
> influencer briefs — passes a **claims checklist** before publishing. AI may
> *draft* copy, but a human runs the checklist and signs off. See file 06 for
> the checklist as an automated gate.

## 2.3 Labeling requirements (the manufacturer must get these right — verify it)

US supplement labels must include, broadly:
- **Statement of identity** (e.g. "Dietary Supplement").
- **Net quantity** of contents.
- **Supplement Facts panel** (serving size, servings per container, ingredients,
  amounts, % Daily Value where established).
- **Ingredient list**, including "other ingredients."
- **Name and place of business** of manufacturer, packer, or distributor.
- **Allergen declarations.**
- The **FDA disclaimer** where structure/function claims are made.

Get a regulatory consultant to **review the label artwork** before the first
print run. A non-compliant label is a recall waiting to happen.

## 2.4 Business structure, liability & insurance

| Item | Recommendation | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Entity** | LLC (US) / Ltd (UK), separate from personal assets | Limit personal liability |
| **Product liability insurance** | **Mandatory before first sale** | Ingestibles = bodily-injury risk; many platforms/retailers require it |
| **General liability** | Yes | Standard cover |
| **Supplier indemnity** | Written into the manufacturing agreement | Manufacturer indemnifies you for their defects |
| **Terms of Service / Privacy Policy** | Lawyer-drafted, on site at launch | Required; GDPR/CCPA exposure from email/SMS marketing |
| **Subscription terms** | Clear, compliant with auto-renewal laws (e.g. US ROSCA / "click-to-cancel", EU consumer law) | Auto-renew "dark patterns" draw FTC action and chargebacks |

> **Click-to-cancel / auto-renewal compliance is critical.** Hard-to-cancel
> subscriptions trigger chargebacks (which threaten your payment processor) and
> regulatory action. Make cancellation genuinely easy. It also improves trust
> and, counterintuitively, retention.

## 2.5 Payments & platform risk

- **Supplements are a "high-risk" category** for many payment processors.
  Some (e.g. certain PayPal/Stripe verticals, and "free trial + auto-ship"
  models especially) restrict or freeze them.
- **Avoid the "free trial → surprise auto-ship" model entirely** — it is the
  classic supplement scam pattern, gets accounts frozen, and invites
  enforcement. Sell honestly: clear price, clear subscription, easy cancel.
- Have a **backup processor** and read the acceptable-use policy *before*
  building on a platform.

## 2.6 Compliance checklist before you sell a single unit

- [ ] Entity formed; business bank account opened.
- [ ] Attorney + regulatory consultant retained.
- [ ] Supplier cGMP cert verified; manufacturing & indemnity agreement signed.
- [ ] Per-batch **Certificate of Analysis (CoA)** process in place.
- [ ] Label artwork reviewed and approved by regulatory consultant.
- [ ] Product liability + general liability insurance bound.
- [ ] Claims checklist created; all copy reviewed against it.
- [ ] FDA structure/function claim notification process understood.
- [ ] ToS, Privacy Policy, Subscription/auto-renewal terms published.
- [ ] Payment processor approved for supplement vertical; backup identified.
- [ ] Recall/adverse-event reporting plan documented (you must report serious
      adverse events — understand the requirement with counsel).

**If any box is unchecked, you do not sell yet.** This is the equivalent of the
trading rule "if the trend isn't confirmed, you stay out."

## 2.7 Cosmetics / Skincare — a SEPARATE regulatory regime

> Adding skincare roughly **doubles your compliance surface**. Skincare is
> regulated as **cosmetics**, not supplements — different rules, different
> claim traps. Read this before listing a single serum.

### The core distinction: cosmetic vs. drug
- A **cosmetic** is intended to cleanse or beautify / change appearance
  (moisturise, smooth the look of, brighten the look of).
- A product becomes a **drug** the moment it claims to affect the body's
  **structure or function** or to **treat/prevent a condition**. A drug needs
  FDA approval — which you will not have. This is the cosmetics equivalent of a
  supplement disease claim, and just as business-ending.

### Skincare claims — NEVER say (these make it an unapproved drug):
- "Treats / cures / heals" anything; "repairs the skin barrier," "regenerates,"
  "rebuilds collagen," "stimulates collagen production."
- "Anti-bacterial / anti-fungal / anti-inflammatory / antiseptic."
- "Treats acne / eczema / psoriasis / rosacea / dermatitis."
- **Sunscreen / SPF / "protects against UV"** — sunscreen is an **OTC drug**
  with its own monograph; do not sell it as a cosmetic.
- "Botox-like," "filler alternative," "penetrates to repair."

### Skincare claims — ALLOWED (appearance / beauty claims):
- "Hydrates," "moisturises," "smooths the look of skin," "for radiant-looking
  skin," "improves the appearance of dullness," "reduces the **appearance** of
  fine lines."
- The rule of thumb baked into our claims gate: structural words like
  "wrinkles," "firms," "lifts" are only acceptable when framed as
  **appearance** ("reduces the appearance of…", "for firmer-**looking** skin").

### US — MoCRA (Modernization of Cosmetics Regulation Act, 2022)
The biggest recent change. With counsel, confirm your obligations, which may
include:
- **Facility registration** and **product listing** with the FDA.
- A **responsible person** named on labels.
- **Safety substantiation** records for each product.
- **Adverse-event** recordkeeping and serious-adverse-event reporting.
- **Fragrance allergen** labeling (as rules finalise).

### EU/UK cosmetics (if selling there)
- **EU Cosmetics Regulation 1223/2009 / UK Cosmetics Regulation**: every
  product needs a **Cosmetic Product Safety Report (CPSR)** by a qualified
  assessor, a **Responsible Person** in-market, **CPNP/SCPN notification**, GMP
  (ISO 22716), and strict ingredient/labeling rules. This is **stricter** than
  the US — budget for it.

### Skincare label essentials (manufacturer/assessor must verify)
Identity, net quantity, **full INCI ingredient list**, responsible-person name
& address, batch code, period-after-opening / best-before, warnings, and
"for external use only." No supplement-style FDA disclaimer (that's a
supplement-only artifact).

### Added checklist items for skincare
- [ ] Cosmetics manufacturer is **ISO 22716 / cosmetic-GMP** compliant.
- [ ] **Safety assessment** on file (MoCRA substantiation / EU CPSR).
- [ ] MoCRA facility registration + product listing (US) / CPNP-SCPN (EU/UK).
- [ ] **Responsible person** named on labels.
- [ ] INCI ingredient list + allergens correct on artwork.
- [ ] No drug claims anywhere (run skincare copy through the *cosmetic* gate).
- [ ] Confirm any "active" (e.g. retinol %, acid %) is at a cosmetic, not drug,
      level and is permitted in each market.

> The automated claims gate (`api/claims.py`) now screens **two regimes**:
> supplement (structure/function + disclaimer) and cosmetic (appearance-only, no
> drug claims). Every product's copy is audited against the right one on server
> start — but a human + counsel still sign off.
