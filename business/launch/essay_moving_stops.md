## Why you keep moving your stop-loss (and how to stop)

You already know the rule. Set the stop before you enter, and if the
price hits it, you're out. Simple. You've read it in every book.

And yet. The trade goes red, the price drifts toward your stop, and a
voice starts up: *it'll bounce back. The market's just shaking out weak
hands. I'll give it a bit more room.* You drag the stop down. Sometimes
it does bounce, and the voice feels vindicated. And then one day it
doesn't bounce — it keeps going — and the small loss you planned for has
quietly become a large one you didn't.

That gap, between knowing the rule and following it, is where most retail
accounts actually die. Not from bad analysis. From good analysis
undermined by a bad moment.

Here's the thing worth sitting with: **the version of you that set the
stop and the version of you that moves it are not the same person.** The
first one was calm, looking at a chart, thinking clearly about risk. The
second one is watching money evaporate in real time, flooded with the
exact chemicals evolution gave you to avoid loss. Asking that second
person to "just be disciplined" is like asking someone to be rational
mid-argument. Willpower is the wrong tool, because willpower is precisely
what the moment steals from you.

So the fix isn't more willpower. It's removing the decision from the
moment entirely.

**Put the stop where your calm self can't reach it.** Concretely: when you
enter, send the stop-loss to your broker in the same order — a bracket or
OCO order. Now the stop isn't a note in your head that you can quietly
revise; it's a live instruction sitting at the exchange. To move it, you
have to consciously cancel a real order, which is a much higher bar than
just... not doing anything. The friction is the feature.

A few things that help make it stick:

- **Decide the stop as a rule, not a feeling.** "Just below the last swing
  low" is a rule. "Where it feels safe" is a feeling that will move with
  your mood. A rule can be automated; a feeling can't.
- **Size the position off the stop, not the other way around.** If the
  honest stop makes the position feel too big to risk, the answer is fewer
  shares — never a tighter, dishonest stop jammed under the current price
  to justify a bigger bet.
- **Judge yourself on process, not outcome.** A trade that hit its stop
  for a planned 1% loss is a *good* trade — you followed the plan. A trade
  that made money because you moved the stop and got lucky is a *bad*
  trade that happened to pay. Keep score that way and the incentive
  flips.

None of this is about being a robot. It's about letting the calm version
of you — the one who's actually good at this — make the decisions, and
building enough structure that the panicking version can't overrule them.

That's the whole game, really. Not predicting the market. Just reliably
doing the sensible thing you already know how to do, on the day it's
hardest.
